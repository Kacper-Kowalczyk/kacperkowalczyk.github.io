Publications

'Transfinitely transitive value', forthcoming in The Philosophical Quarterly. [abstract]

Abstract: This paper develops transfinite extensions of transitivity and acyclicity in the context of population ethics. They are used to argue that it is better to add good lives, worse to add bad lives, and equally good to add neutral lives, where a life’s value is understood as personal value. These conclusions rule out a number of theories of population ethics, feed into an argument for the repugnant conclusion, and allow us to reduce different number comparisons to same number ones. Challenges to these arguments are addressed, including the issue of comparing existence and nonexistence in terms of personal value, the possibility of minimal quanta of time and life, and the meaningfulness of measuring closeness between outcomes with different population sizes. An asymmetry is uncovered between transfinite cycles of worseness and betterness, supporting a version of the weak procreative asymmetry. Transfinite transitivity principles are also favourably compared to the better known principles of continuity.

The risky existential question and the repugnant conclusion (joint work with Michal Masny) [abstract]

Abstract: In "An Intrapersonal Addition Paradox" (2019), Jake Nebel presents a novel argument for the Repugnant Conclusion which is meant to be more compelling than the existing ones. This paper develops a range of responses to this argument. Along the way, it addresses a number of issues whose importance extends to the whole field of population ethics: the individual value of prospects involving a chance of nonexistence, the adequacy of perfectionism, the "containers of value" objection, and the relevance of the ex ante/ex post distinction in variable-population contexts.

Allais preferences are bad for you [abstract]

Abstract: There has been a long debate about the rationality of Allais preferences. This paper is about the ethics of Allais preferences. It shows that if Allais preferences track individual betterness, then we cannot hold on to independently plausible ethical principles: ex ante Pareto, statewise dominance and anonymity. The paper considers the egalitarian challenge to this combination of principles and shows that it does not apply here. There is little other reason to doubt their current application. The suggested conclusion is that Allais preferences cannot track individual betterness after all. In short, Allais preferences are bad for you.

Egalitarianism and population size [abstract]

Abstract: There are two kinds of egalitarianism: communal and weighted. According to the former, the value of an outcome is the result of balancing total wellbeing and the badness of inequality. According to the latter, it is the result of aggregating people’s wellbeing while giving priority to the relatively worse off. Unlike communal egalitarianism, weighted egalitarianism escapes the levelling down objection and can be supported by a veil of ignorance argument. While the two kinds of egalitarianism can generate the same verdicts in same number cases, they diverge in different number cases in a way which poses a significant dilemma for weighted egalitarians. On the one hand, they have good reasons to accept limited principles of existence independence which leads to a specific formula for calculating the value of an outcome. On the other hand, the most straightforward application of this formula in different number cases yields counterintuitive verdicts which are at odds with egalitarian thinking. The dilemma suggests that weighted egalitarianism is not egalitarian, after all, so egalitarians should accept communal egalitarianism instead.

Risk times infinity [abstract]

Abstract: This paper introduces a new puzzle for utilitarianism in infinite worlds. It shows that ex ante Pareto and statewise dominance are incompatible with a nearly uncontroversial utilitarian claim about outcomes where infinitely many people exist. The three principles at issue are then clarified and defended. The incompatibility means that utilitarianism is ex ante/ex post inconsistent in infinite worlds. It is well known that ex ante/ex post inconsistency is a source of problems for egalitarians and prioritarians in finite worlds. It turns out that utilitarians share these problems. The paper then explains how the problem is an example of an “infinite free lunch” and how it is related to other problem cases for utilitarianism in infinite worlds.

Thesis

'Persons, populations, and value' [abstract]

Abstract: This thesis consists of six independent papers on personal identity, population ethics, and value theory. The central theme of this thesis is the unimportance of personal identity. I begin in Chapter 1 by developing new Parfitian arguments for the unimportance of personal identity in morality, paying special attention to their implications in population ethics. In Chapter 2 I analyse Mark Johnston’s similar metaphysics driven challenge to person based morality, but argue that it is less successful than my own. In Chapter 3 I lay out choices facing egalitarians in population ethics, arguing against some recently prominent forms of egalitarianism. In Chapter 4 I provide new decision theoretic arguments against deontic constraints on harming, suggesting that it is consequentialism rather than deontology that can better respect persons. In Chapter 5 I introduce transfinite extensions of the familiar value theoretic principles of transitivity and acyclicity. I use them to try to resolve some key issues in population ethics, concerning the value of creating new people and the procreative asymmetry. In Chapter 6 I aim to support these transfinite principles by analysing their role in the theory of rational choice.
